FACT SHEET ON COPERNICUS IN SITU DATA REQUIREMENTS

The Copernicus Emergency Management Service

Rapid Mapping Component

The Copernicus Services rely on a combination of satellite data and environmental measurements collected from ground-based, sea-borne or air-borne monitoring systems, as well as geospatial reference or ancillary data. These non-space data are collectively referred to as “in situ” data. This Fact Sheet is one of a series which summarise the in situ data requirements for the Copernicus Services.

What in situ data is required for the component?

The Copernicus EMS Mapping component requires a range of basic reference data on topography (natural land surface and man-made features) as well as risk-related information (population distribution, event probability). It also relies on the availability of geometrically corrected imagery (orthoimagery) and a digital elevation model (DEM) of the affected or risk-prone areas. Datasets with global coverage, when existing, are preferred; alternatively, regional or national datasets are taken into consideration.

REFERENCE TOPOGRAPHIC DATASETS

These are mainly vector datasets that describe the normal condition (i.e. pre-crisis) of the territory and assets in the area of interest, including both natural and manmade features.

Settlements, transport networks and related infrastructures, industry and utilities, are together referred to as “assets” and have priority. Their elements should be accessible through direct download or standard Web Services (e.g. WFS), classified according to principal use.

PRE and/or POST EVENT ORTHOIMAGERY

This refers to geo-referenced and geometrically corrected imagery of the area of interest, from either satellite or airborne sensors.

  • Ground Sample Distance (GSD) >= 0.1 m
  • Orthoimagery should be accessible through direct download or standard Web Services (e.g. WMS or WCS)
  • Recent acquisition (generally no more than 3 years)
  • Natural RGB colours. False colours (exploiting the Near Infrared information) may be helpful for thematic information extraction.

DIGITAL ELEVATION MODEL (DEM)

The representation of continuous elevation values over a topographic surface by a regular array of height values, referenced to a common vertical datum.

  • Ground Sample Distance (GSD), resolution and vertical accuracy suitable for satellite imagery ortho-correction.
  • DEM should be accessible through direct download.

RISK-RELATED DATA

Datasets related to the risk of natural disasters or other hazards, asset vulnerability and other datasets to be used as input to models are needed in order to generate products for the Risk & Recovery Mapping Module.

Challenges and opportunities

  • The main challenge is accessing and exploiting in situ data within emergency management timeframe (delivery within hours to days). Service providers need information in advance (access details, data formats/models) to develop ad-hoc ETL procedures. In some cases, access must be granted by the NMCA, and technical and infrastructure-related restrictions (downtime, file size limits) also play a role.
  • For reference topographic datasets and pre-event aerial orthoimagery, global datasets are often insufficient (coverage, accuracy, authoritativeness, licensing). At global level, the challenge is the lack of consistent, accessible datasets with full coverage fit-for-use format (single building features). For example, OpenStreetMap data describes assets at global level, but often they’re incomplete and sources are not authoritative; better alternative datasets are provided by several NMCAs in Europe. The goal would be that all countries make their NMCA datasets accessible, fulfilling the emergency timeframe.
  • Whilst local in situ data may exist, they are either inaccessible, not accessible in the correct timeframe, or are made available in an inappropriate format (e.g. raster vs. vector).
  • The availability and the quality of in situ data be substantially improved by building on the lessons learned from ongoing initiatives and projects, such as the European Environment Agency - EuroGeographics agreement, CORDA (Copernicus Reference Data Access) and ELF (European Location Framework).
